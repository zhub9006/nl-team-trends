## Founding Era (1876–1900) <a id="founding-era"></a>

### Overview
The National League was established on February 2, 1876, as the first fully professional baseball league to survive. It replaced the National Association (1871–1875), which had struggled with instability, franchise churn, and lack of central authority.

### Original 8 Teams (1876)
| Team | Modern Equivalent | City |
|------|-------------------|------|
| Boston Red Caps | Atlanta Braves | Boston → Milwaukee → Atlanta |
| Chicago White Stockings | Chicago Cubs | Chicago |
| Cincinnati Reds | Cincinnati Reds | Cincinnati |
| Hartford Dark Blues | — | Hartford → Brooklyn → N/A |
| Louisville Grays | — | Louisville |
| Philadelphia Athletics | — | Philadelphia |
| Brooklyn Mutuals | — | Brooklyn |
| St. Louis Browns | — | St. Louis |

### Key Trends
- **Extreme competitive dominance by single teams**: Chicago won 5 of 8 inaugural-season pennants; Boston won 4 of 7 from 1877–1882
- **Small league (8 teams)**: Limited competition but rapid establishment of professional standards
- **Schedule variability**: 60 games in 1876 growing to 84–136 by 1900
- **Franchise churn**: 6 of original 8 teams folded or were expelled by 1882; league stabilized to 8 teams by 1883
- **Denver/Beaneaters dynasty**: Boston won 9 pennants from 1877–1898
- **Baltimore Orioles**: Dominated 1894–1898 with 5 consecutive pennants

### Notable Records
- **Best season**: 1884 Providence Grays — 84-28 (.750) in 112 games
- **Most consecutive pennants**: Boston Beaneaters — 5 (1891–1895, 1897–1899)
- **First perfect season**: 1869 Cincinnati Red Stockings (pre-NL, NA)

### Era Win% Leaders
- Boston Red Caps (1876–1882): .670
- Chicago White Stockings (1876–1900): .642
- Baltimore Orioles (1882–1899): .635

## Dead-Ball Era (1901–1919) <a id="dead-ball-era"></a>

### Overview
The early 1900s saw the NL stabilize at 8 teams while facing competition from the American League (founded 1901). This era was characterized by low-scoring games, dominant pitching, and record-setting team performances.

### Key Trends
- **Cubs dynasty (1906–1910)**: 5 consecutive NL championship appearances, 4 pennants, 2 WS titles
- **Record-setting team wins**: 1906 Cubs won 116 games (all-time NL record)
- **Giants power**: NY Giants won 4 pennants from 1911–1913
- **Pirates consistency**: Pittsburgh made 5 consecutive postseason appearances (1901–1909)
- **Miracle Braves (1914)**: Went from last place on July 4 to World Series champions
- **1919 season**: Only 140 games due to Woodrow Wilson's "Work or Fight" order

### Notable Records
- **Most wins in a season**: 1906 Cubs — 116-36 (.763)
- **Best win% in a season**: 1899 Brooklyn Superbas — .678 (Dead-Ball era equivalent)
- **Most consecutive pennants**: NY Giants — 3 (1911–1913)

### Era Win% Leaders
- Chicago Cubs (1901–1919): .638
- NY Giants (1901–1919): .601
- Pittsburgh Pirates (1901–1919): .588

## Pre-War Power (1920–1940) <a id="pre-war-power"></a>

### Overview
The 1920s through early 1940s saw the NL's competitive map shift dramatically. The Cardinals emerged as a powerhouse while the Giants remained competitive. Franchise relocations began to reshape the league's geography.

### Key Trends
- **Cardinals rise**: Won their first WS title in 1926; 5 pennants from 1926–1934; 3 straight WS titles 1942–1944
- **NY Giants dominance**: 4 pennants from 1921–1924; 1951 Bobby Thomson walk-off HR
- **Dodgers relocation (1958)**: Brooklyn moved to Los Angeles, fundamentally altering the NL competitive map
- **Giants relocation (1958)**: NY moved to San Francisco, creating the modern NL West
- **Braves relocation (1953)**: Boston → Milwaukee, first franchise move since 1903
- **Growing South**: expansion into new markets with Milwaukee, then later Houston, Atlanta

### Notable Records
- **1906 Cubs**: 116-36 (.763) — all-time NL record
- **1935 Cubs**: 100-54 (.649) — best season of the 1930s
- **1931 Cardinals**: 101-53 (.655) — best Cardinals season of the era

### Era Win% Leaders
- St. Louis Cardinals (1920–1940): .575
- NY Giants (1920–1940): .567
- Chicago Cubs (1920–1940): .556

## Cardinals Dynasty (1941–1960) <a id="cardinals-dynasty"></a>

### Overview
The St. Louis Cardinals entered the most dominant dynasty in NL history during this period. They won 9 pennants and 6 World Series titles from 1941–1967, including one of the greatest dynasties in all of North American professional sports.

### Key Trends
- **Cardinals dynasty**: 5 straight pennants (1942–1946), 3 straight WS titles (1942–1944); total of 9 pennants and 6 WS titles from 1941–1967
- **1944 "Streetcar Series"**: Cardinals vs. Cardinals in WS (both St. Louis teams)
- **Dodgers challenger**: Brooklyn became a true powerhouse in the 1950s
- **Giants "Shot Heard 'Round the World"**: 1951 NL pennant via Bobby Thomson's walk-off HR
- **Relocation era**: 1958 — Brooklyn → LA, NY Giants → SF
- **Braves move**: Milwaukee (1953) became competitive but eventually moved to Atlanta (1966)
- **Expansion**: Houston Colt .45s (1962), New York Mets (1962), Montreal Expos (1969), San Diego Padres (1969)

### Notable Records
- **Most consecutive pennants**: Cardinals — 5 (1942–1946)
- **Most WS titles in an era**: Cardinals — 6 (1941–1967)
- **1951 NY Giants**: Greatest pennant race in baseball history

### Era Win% Leaders
- St. Louis Cardinals (1941–1960): .563
- Brooklyn/LA Dodgers (1941–1960): .540
- NY/SF Giants (1941–1960): .512

## Expansion Era (1961–1980) <a id="expansion-era"></a>

### Overview
The NL expanded from 10 to 12 teams in 1962, then split into divisions in 1969. This era saw the Mets' Miracle 1969, the Big Red Machine, and the beginning of the Braves' modern dynasty.

### Key Trends
- **1962 expansion**: Houston Colt .45s and NY Mets join; 162-game schedule begins
- **Miracle Mets (1969)**: 100-62 expansion team wins WS against heavily favored Orioles
- **Big Red Machine**: Reds win 2 WS titles (1975, 1976); 4 division titles in 5 years
- **Pirates consistency**: 3 division titles (1970, 1971, 1974, 1979); WS title in 1971, 1979
- **Division format (1969)**: NL East and NL West created; first divisional champions
- **Dodgers**: 5 pennants from 1963–1974 but only 1 WS (1965)
- **1969 expansion**: Montreal Expos and San Diego Padres added

### Notable Records
- **1969 Mets**: Worst-to-first expansion franchise WS title
- **1975–76 Reds**: First NL back-to-back WS titles since 1920s
- **1965 Dodgers**: Won 97 games but finished 2nd (behind Pirates) before 1969 division era

### Era Win% Leaders
- LA Dodgers (1961–1980): .566
- Cincinnati Reds (1961–1980): .553
- Pittsburgh Pirates (1961–1980): .540

## Braves Dynasty (1981–2000) <a id="braves-dynasty"></a>

### Overview
The Atlanta Braves established the most dominant division dynasty in MLB history, winning 14 consecutive NL East division titles from 1991–2005. This era also saw the end of the Cubs' and Phillies' long droughts.

### Key Trends
- **14 consecutive division titles**: 1991–2005 (unprecedented in MLB history)
- **1995 WS title**: Sweep of Indians in World Series
- **1994 strike**: Abruptly ended the season; Braves leading NL East at 68-46 when strike hit
- **1990 Reds**: Ended 14-year WS drought with championship
- **1997 Marlins**: Youngest expansion team wins WS in 7 games
- **1999 Mets**: 97-65 but lost NLCS to Braves
- **1981 division split**: Dodgers won WS despite unusual split-season format

### Notable Records
- **Longest division title streak**: Braves — 14 (1991–2005)
- **1995 Braves**: Swept both postseason rounds; fewest wins to win WS in modern era
- **1994 Expos**: Best record in MLB (74-40) when season was cut short

### Era Win% Leaders
- Atlanta Braves (1981–2000): .588 (division season only)
- Cincinnati Reds (1981–2000): .530
- Pittsburgh Pirates (1981–2000): .512

## Resurgence Cycle (2001–2015) <a id="resurgence-cycle"></a>

### Overview
This era saw the competitive landscape of the NL shift dramatically, with no single team dominating for more than a few years. The Cardinals, Giants, and Diamondbacks all captured championships. The Cubs ended their historic drought.

### Key Trends
- **Cardinals dynasty (2000s)**: 3 NL pennants (2004, 2006, 2011), 2 WS titles (2006, 2011)
- **2001 Diamondbacks**: Youngest franchise to win WS (just 4 years old)
- **2005 Astros**: First team to play in both AL and NL WS (won AL 2005, NL 2005)
- **2006 Cardinals**: Lowest WS-winning regular season wins (83) since 1973
- **2010 Giants**: Third title in 5 years (2010, 2012, 2014)
- **2014 Giants**: 3rd title in 5 years
- **2012 Nationals**: 98-64 but lost Wild Card game
- **2015 Cubs**: Return to WS after 71 years, ultimately winning in 2016

### Notable Records
- **2001 Diamondbacks**: Most wins by an expansion team in first 4 years (389)
- **2004 Cardinals**: 105 wins but lost WS (still best WS-losing record in modern era at the time)
- **2010-2014 Giants**: Most recent NL three-peat (not consecutive years)

### Era Win% Leaders
- St. Louis Cardinals (2001–2015): .553
- LA Dodgers (2001–2015): .531
- Atlanta Braves (2001–2015): .512

## Modern Dodgers Dynasty (2016–2025) <a id="modern-dodgers"></a>

### Overview
The LA Dodgers have established themselves as the dominant force in the NL during this era, winning 3 World Series titles (2016, 2020, 2024-2025), 8 consecutive NL West division titles, and consistently fielding the highest payroll and best record in baseball.

### Key Trends
- **8 consecutive NL West titles**: 2017-2025 (unprecedented dominance of a division)
- **2016 Cubs**: Ended 108-year WS drought; dramatic 7-game WS comeback vs Indians
- **2017 Dodgers**: 104-58 but lost WS to Astros; highest win total in NL in 162-game era
- **2020 Dodgers**: 43-17 (.717) in COVID-shortened season; swept to WS title
- **2022 Dodgers**: 111-51 (.685) — best 162-game NL season ever
- **Back-to-back WS titles**: 2024-2025 (first NL repeat champion since 1998-2000)
- **Dodgers vs Cubs H2H**: 278-122 (.695) — most lopsided modern rivalry
- **Dodgers payroll**: Consistently highest in MLB; payroll advantage over NL rivals

### Notable Records
- **Most consecutive NL West titles**: Dodgers — 8 (2017-2025)
- **Best 162-game NL season**: Dodgers 2022 — 111-51 (.685)
- **Best shortened-season NL record**: Dodgers 2020 — 43-17 (.717)
- **Most WS titles in 5-year span**: Dodgers — 3 of 5 (2020, 2024, 2025)

### Era Win% Leaders
- LA Dodgers (2016–2025): .629
- Atlanta Braves (2016–2025): .540
- Chicago Cubs (2016–2025): .518

## Cross-Era Trends <a id="cross-era-trends"></a>

### 1. Geographic Shifts Reshape the NL
- **1958**: Brooklyn → LA, NY Giants → SF — created the modern NL West
- **1966**: Milwaukee → Atlanta — Braves moved to a new market
- **1969**: Montreal → Washington — Expos moved to become Nationals
- **Key insight**: Every relocation fundamentally reshuffled NL competitive geography

### 2. The Cardinals Are Permanent Contenders
- The Cardinals have been in 10+ NL pennant races per decade since 1926
- Only franchise besides the Cubs to appear in every NL postseason round since 1969
- .553 all-time win% — best among NL's traditional powers (excluding Giants and Dodgers)

### 3. Expansion Creates Competitive Imbalance
- Pre-expansion (1876-1968): Small league, fewer competitive imbalances
- Post-expansion (1969-present): More teams, more wildcard races, but also more revenue disparity
- The Padres, Rockies, Diamondbacks, and Marlins have never matched the Cardinals/Dodgers/Giants payrolls

### 4. Dynasty Patterns
- **Genuine dynasties**: Cardinals (1942-1967), Braves (1991-2005), Dodgers (2016-2025)
- **Short surges**: Cubs (1906-1910), Giants (1921-1924), Reds (1970s), Phillies (2008-2009)
- **Dynasty duration**: The most sustained NL dynasties have all lasted 10+ years and produced 3+ WS titles

### 5. H2H Dynamics
- **Most lopsided modern matchups**: Dodgers vs Cubs (.695), Cardinals vs Pirates (.606)
- **Most competitive**: Mets vs Phillies (50-50), Cardinals vs Phiilies (49.4%)
- **Relocation impact**: Pre-1958 rivalries (Giants-Cubs) were different from post-relocation ones

### 6. Home Market Size & Success
- **Top NL markets**: LA, St. Louis, NY/SF, Chicago — all with large or historically loyal fanbases
- **Smallest NL markets**: Milwaukee, Colorado, San Diego — have struggled to sustain contention
- **Cincinnati anomaly**: Large market but inconsistent performance; 5 WS titles but 14-year WS drought (1976-1990)

### 7. The Cubs Dichotomy
- Best franchise (all-time) in terms of sustained presence and historical significance
- Longest championship drought in North American professional sports (108 years)
- Paradox of massive fanbase + league-worst attendance during drought years (2015 average attendance: 30,869)

### 8. Postseason Format Evolution
- **Pre-1969**: Pennant winner advances directly to WS
- **1969–1993**: Division winners advance to LCS
- **1994–present**: Wild Card added; 3 division winners + WC play in best-of-5 LDS before LCS
- **2022–present**: 6-team playoff format (3 division winners + 3 wild cards); best-of-3 Wild Card Series

### Key Takeaways

1. **The NL is defined by dynasties**: No franchise has sustained success without winning consecutive pennants across a decade-plus span. Each genuine NL dynasty (Cardinals, Braves, Dodgers) has earned 3+ WS titles and dominated for 10+ years.

2. **Geography matters — but relocation reshapes it**: The 1958 moves of the Dodgers and Giants fundamentally altered the NL competitive landscape, creating the modern NL West and ending New York's NL dominance.

3. **Expansion has diluted but not destroyed competitiveness**: While small-market teams still struggle, the 2001 Diamondbacks and 2016 Cubs show that a well-run expansion team can compete with the best.

4. **The Cardinals are the NL's most consistent contender**: With 11 WS titles, 19 pennants, and .513 all-time win%, they are the NL's Gold Standard.

5. **The Dodgers are the modern NL dynasty**: Since 2016, they have been the most dominant team in baseball — 3 WS titles, 8 straight division titles, and an all-time .532 win%.

6. **The Cubs' story is uniquely MLB**: The 108-year drought remains the longest in North American professional sports, and their 2016 championship is considered one of the greatest upsets in sports history.
